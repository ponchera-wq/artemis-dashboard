Orion is the crew transport vehicle for Artemis III, carrying four astronauts to low Earth orbit for rendezvous and docking with commercial Human Landing Systems. Built by Lockheed Martin with a European Service Module from Airbus/ESA, Orion has a revised heat shield addressing AVCOAT permeability issues observed on Artemis I and accepted on Artemis II. The crew module performs the critical docking maneuvers with HLS vehicles in orbit.

MISSION ROLE

On Artemis III, Orion is the primary crew vehicle, launching from Kennedy Space Center atop SLS and reaching low Earth orbit. From LEO, Orion performs rendezvous maneuvers with Starship HLS and/or Blue Moon Mk2 (which launch separately on commercial boosters and refuel in orbit). The spacecraft executes docking, validates crew transfer interfaces, and serves as the safe haven for the crew during all orbital operations. Orion then returns to Earth, splashing down in the Pacific Ocean after re-entry.

HEAT SHIELD UPDATES

AVCOAT Permeability Issue

Artemis I's Orion heat shield exhibited higher-than-expected permeability in the AVCOAT ablative material after flight. Post-flight analysis showed localized moisture ingress and material degradation in specific areas. This was accepted for Artemis II (2026) with flight data monitoring; the mission flew successfully with the existing shield design.

Artemis III Heat Shield

For Artemis III, engineers have implemented targeted improvements to AVCOAT formulation and manufacturing process controls, reducing permeability while maintaining ablation performance. The shield retains the 16.5 ft diameter and 186 Avcoat blocks bonded to a titanium skeleton. Revised bonding procedures and material composition address the root causes identified post-Artemis I.

KEY SYSTEMS FOR ARTEMIS III

Docking System

Orion retains its International Docking System (IDS) interface for mechanical and electrical connection to HLS vehicles. The system was validated during Artemis II proximity operations with the ICPS upper stage and is ready for full docking cycles on Artemis III.

Rendezvous Navigation

Advanced GPS and relative navigation sensors enable autonomous or crew-guided rendezvous with Starship and Blue Moon. The spacecraft can operate through signal loss and maintain stable proximity to the HLS vehicle while docked or during undocking maneuvers.

Life Support & Environmental Control

The regenerable CO₂ scrubbing system requires no consumable chemicals and operates reliably in microgravity for the ~10-15 day Artemis III mission. High-pressure oxygen and nitrogen tanks support crew comfort and emergency breathing.

Crew Seats & Restraints

Four adjustable seats with 5-point harnesses accommodate the 1st–99th percentile of human body sizes. Seats are rated for launch, landing, and emergency abort scenarios.

EUROPEAN SERVICE MODULE

The cylindrical, unpressurized service module (provided by ESA, built by Airbus) delivers propulsion, power, and consumables. Four large solar arrays provide ~11 kW continuous power. The AJ10 main engine (6,000 lbf) performs orbital maneuvers; auxiliary thrusters handle attitude control. The service module separates before crew module re-entry.
