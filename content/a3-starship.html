SpaceX's Starship HLS is a lunar variant of the Starship upper stage, stripped of atmospheric flight hardware (heat shield, flaps, etc.). It launches separately from SLS on SpaceX's Super Heavy booster, refuels in Earth orbit from approximately 10–16 tanker flights, then boosts itself to lunar orbit for rendezvous with Orion on Artemis III. Orbital propellant transfer demonstration is targeted for 2026; Starship V3 (first orbital-refueling-capable variant) is scheduled for maiden flight NET May 2026.

DESIGN APPROACH

Unlike traditional purpose-built landers, Starship HLS leverages the same flight-proven Starship architecture used for cargo and crewed transport missions. The lunar variant is optimized by removing atmospheric entry and hover/landing hardware. Avionics, propulsion, and life support are adapted from existing Starship/Dragon heritage systems, reducing development cost and schedule risk.

PROPULSION & FUELING

Raptor Engines

Starship HLS is powered by Raptor 3 engines (next-generation variant). On the lunar variant, engines are optimized for long-duration vacuum operations. The lander carries sufficient fuel to boost from Earth orbit (~250 nm) to lunar orbit and perform landing/ascent burns.

Orbital Refueling

The Starship HLS launches with a fraction of its fuel capacity. In Earth orbit, it rendezvous with and receives fuel from 10–16 dedicated Starship tanker flights. These tankers also launch on Super Heavy, refuel the HLS, and return to Earth. The refueling fleet is positioned in a circular Earth orbit before Orion and the HLS rendezvous.

CRITICAL MILESTONES

Starship V3 & Orbital Refueling (2026)

SpaceX is developing Starship V3, a variant optimized for orbital propellant transfer operations. V3's maiden flight is targeted for NET May 2026, with refueling demonstrations to follow. These tests are essential to validate the orbital logistics architecture required for Artemis III and beyond.

Cryogenic Propellant Storage in Space

Long-duration cryogenic storage is the single greatest technical risk. Liquid hydrogen boils away at 20 K; liquid oxygen at 90 K. Starship HLS must maintain propellant integrity for days of coasting in Earth orbit and during lunar transit. Advanced insulation, zero-boil-off systems, and active thermal management are under development.

Crew Interfaces

On Artemis III, the lander will dock with Orion and transfer crew between vessels. Airlock design, suit interfaces, and contingency abort procedures are being validated by SpaceX and NASA jointly.

ARTEMIS III ROLE

Starship HLS on Artemis III will launch separately, perform Earth orbit refueling (or demonstrate refueling readiness), and rendezvous with Orion in LEO. Crew will transfer to the lander, tour systems, validate docking interfaces, and demonstrate contingency procedures — all without leaving Earth orbit. This de-risks both the vehicle and crew procedures before lunar operations on Artemis IV.
