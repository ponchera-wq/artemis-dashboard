Artemis III is a crewed demonstration mission in low Earth orbit designed to test rendezvous and docking with commercial Human Landing Systems before committing them to lunar operations. The mission was revised in February 2026 when NASA moved the first crewed lunar landing to Artemis IV. Four astronauts will launch aboard SLS/Orion, perform proximity operations with one or both commercial HLS vehicles in orbit, and test the Axiom AxEMU spacesuit — broadly comparable to Apollo 9 (1969).

MISSION OVERVIEW

Artemis III represents a fundamental shift in lunar exploration architecture. Rather than following the original plan for a direct lunar landing, NASA has restructured the program to leverage two independent commercial Human Landing System providers — SpaceX and Blue Origin — reducing cost and increasing redundancy. Artemis III serves as the in-orbit demonstration flight before either lander carries crew to the lunar surface.

The mission profile closely mirrors Apollo 9 (May 1969), which tested the Lunar Module in Earth orbit and validated rendezvous procedures before the Moon landings. On Artemis III, the crew will rendezvous with Starship HLS and/or Blue Moon Mk2 in LEO, performing docking operations and systems checks without venturing beyond Earth orbit.

MISSION PROFILE

MISSION DURATION ~10-15 days (estimated)
CREW SIZE 4 astronauts
OPERATIONAL ALTITUDE Low Earth Orbit (~250 nm)
TARGET LAUNCH WINDOW NET July 1, 2027 (tentative)
LANDING SYSTEM No surface landing (orbital demo)

PRIMARY OBJECTIVES

Demonstrate rendezvous and docking with commercial Human Landing Systems in Earth orbit

Validate docking and undocking procedures with HLS vehicles

Perform AxEMU spacesuit qualification testing and evaluation

Evaluate Orion crew interfaces for lunar operations

Validate HLS-to-Orion crew transfer procedures

Reduce risk for Artemis IV lunar surface operations

WHY ARTEMIS III CHANGED

The original Artemis III architecture called for a direct lunar landing demonstration. In February 2026, NASA restructured the program to reduce schedule and cost risk while maintaining momentum on two competing HLS providers. Moving the first landing to Artemis IV allows SpaceX and Blue Origin additional time for uncrewed demonstration flights and orbital validation before committing crew to the lunar surface. This approach is grounded in decades of experience with multi-vehicle, human-rated missions.
