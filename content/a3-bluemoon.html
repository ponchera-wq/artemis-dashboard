Blue Origin is developing two distinct Blue Moon variants for lunar cargo and crewed operations. Mk1 is a 3-tonne cargo lander launching on New Glenn in 2026 for a Pathfinder uncrewed mission. Mk2 is a 15-meter-tall crewed lander designed to carry up to four astronauts for 30-day lunar surface stays, powered by BE-7 engines and requiring a separate "Cislunar Transporter" tug built by Lockheed Martin. Mk2's uncrewed lunar demonstration is planned for 2027.

BLUE MOON MK1 — CARGO VARIANT

Mission Profile

Mk1 is a high-energy cargo lander optimized to deliver up to 3 tonnes of payload to the lunar surface. It fits within the New Glenn 7-meter payload fairing and launches as a secondary payload on Blue Origin's heavy-lift vehicle. The Pathfinder uncrewed mission is scheduled for 2026, demonstrating landing accuracy and cargo deployment without crew risk.

Design

Mk1 uses proven Blue Origin propulsion (derivatives of New Shepard heritage) and avionics. The lander is compact and lightweight, focused on reliability and simplicity for cargo delivery. Landing legs are engineered for diverse lunar terrain. A sensor suite validates landing zone conditions and payload deployment.

BLUE MOON MK2 — CREWED VARIANT

Architecture

Mk2 is a 15-meter-tall pressurized lander with a large cabin, accommodating four astronauts for extended lunar surface operations lasting up to 30 days. Unlike Mk1, Mk2 requires in-space cryogenic propellant transfer from a dedicated tug for lunar trajectory and descent. The lander is not standalone; mission architecture depends on the Cislunar Transporter for fuel delivery.

Propulsion

Mk2 is powered by BE-7 engines burning liquid hydrogen (LH2) and liquid oxygen (LOX). BE-7 is an evolution of the BE-4 engine (used on Vulcan and Centaur) optimized for vacuum operations. The dual-engine design provides redundancy and sufficient thrust for orbit-to-surface descent and ascent burns.

Crew Accommodation

The pressurized cabin provides ~200 m³ of habitable volume, supporting four crew with sleeping quarters, galley, waste management, and exercise facilities for up to 30 days. Life support systems include regenerative CO₂ scrubbing and water recovery. Airlock design facilitates EVA operations in lunar and gravity environments.

CISLUNAR TRANSPORTER — THE FUEL TUG

Contractor & Role

Lockheed Martin is developing the Cislunar Transporter, a reusable spacecraft that launches separately and rendezvous with Blue Moon Mk2 in Earth orbit. The tug performs in-space propellant transfer (LH2 and LOX) to the lander, then boosts it to lunar trajectory. Without the Cislunar Transporter, Mk2 cannot reach the Moon.

Propellant Transfer

Cryogenic propellant transfer in space is operationally complex. The tug must rendezvous with Mk2, dock, manage boil-off during transfer, and monitor tank pressures and temperatures. This capability is being developed in partnership between Blue Origin and Lockheed Martin and is a critical path item for Mk2 readiness.

ARTEMIS III DEMONSTRATION

MISSION ROLE In-orbit rendezvous and docking with Orion
LAUNCH VEHICLE New Glenn (Blue Origin) or equivalent
DOCKING INTERFACE International Docking System (IDS)
DEMONSTRATION SCOPE Orbital operations only (no lunar landing)

On Artemis III, Blue Moon Mk2 (or Mk1 adapted) will launch on New Glenn, rendezvous with Orion in low Earth orbit, and dock via the International Docking System. Crew will transfer to the lander, perform system checks, and validate procedures — all in orbit. This demonstration reduces risk before the uncrewed lunar test flight (2027) and eventual crewed Artemis IV mission.
