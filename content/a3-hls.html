NASA has contracted two independent Human Landing System providers to enable crewed lunar surface access starting with Artemis IV: SpaceX (Starship HLS) and Blue Origin (Blue Moon Mk2). The revised February 2026 program uses Artemis III as a critical demonstration flight where both landers (or at least one) perform orbital operations and rendezvous with Orion in low Earth orbit before progressing to lunar missions.

DUAL-PROVIDER STRATEGY

NASA selected two independent HLS contractors to maximize reliability, reduce cost through competition, and ensure crew has options if one system encounters technical difficulties. SpaceX was contracted in April 2021 and originally planned for Artemis III; Blue Origin was contracted in September 2023 for Artemis V. The February 2026 program restructuring moved both companies forward by using Artemis III as an in-orbit demonstration before lunar operations begin on Artemis IV.

ARTEMIS III DEMONSTRATION PHASE

Rather than landing on the Moon, Artemis III serves as a validation mission in low Earth orbit. One or both HLS vehicles will launch on commercial boosters, rendezvous with Orion at altitude ~250 nm, and demonstrate docking and crew transfer procedures. This approach is grounded in proven methodology: Apollo 9 (1969) tested the Lunar Module in Earth orbit before Apollo 10 and 11 landed on the Moon. Artemis III allows both contractors to demonstrate readiness without the added complexity and risk of lunar surface operations.

HLS REQUIREMENTS

CREW CAPACITY Up to 4 astronauts (lunar variant)
SURFACE DURATION 30+ day stays (design target for Mk2)
PROPELLANT STORAGE Cryogenic (LH2/LOX) in space
DOCKING INTERFACE International Docking System (IDS)
UNCREWED DEMO Required before lunar crew operations

CRITICAL DEPENDENCIES

Orbital Propellant Transfer

Both HLS variants rely on in-space cryogenic propellant transfer — either from tanker spacecraft (Starship) or from a dedicated tug (Blue Moon Cislunar Transporter). This capability has not yet been demonstrated at scale. SpaceX is targeting an orbital refueling demonstration in 2026; Blue Origin's Lockheed Martin-built Cislunar Transporter is under development.

Cryogenic Storage & Boil-off

Long-duration missions require landers to store cryogenic propellants (liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen) in space for days or weeks with minimal boil-off. This is a mature technology for short missions but has not been fully validated for the long dwell times required for lunar surface operations.

Crew Interface & Certification

Both systems must be certified for crew transport, including fail-safe abort capability, life support, and safe haven provisions. Artemis III provides the first opportunity to validate crew interfaces in a human-rated environment.

POST-ARTEMIS III ROADMAP

Following Artemis III orbital validation, each HLS contractor must complete an uncrewed lunar demonstration flight before carrying crew to the surface. Artemis IV (targeting 2029 or beyond) will be the first crewed lunar surface mission, with one of the two HLS systems delivering astronauts to the lunar South Pole region for approximately one week.
