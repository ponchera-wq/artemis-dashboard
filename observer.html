LOCAL OBSERVING CONDITIONS — GUIDE

Five factors determine whether your sky is ready for astrophotography. The verdict combines all of them into a single readiness rating.

☁ Cloud Cover
The most critical factor. Even thin cirrus scatters light and kills contrast. Any cloud above 20% will measurably degrade your images — above 60% the sky is effectively blocked.
0–20%Excellent transparency
20–60%Patchy — session at risk
60–100%Overcast — no-go
💨 Wind Speed
Wind drives atmospheric turbulence ("seeing") that blurs star images even in a perfectly clear sky. At higher speeds your mount will also struggle to track and long exposures will smear.
<10 km/hSteady seeing — optimal
10–25 km/hMarginal seeing
>25 km/hPoor seeing / tracking issues
💧 Humidity
High humidity reduces sky transparency through haze and dramatically increases dew risk on exposed optics. Mirrors, lenses, and camera sensors can fog within minutes above 80%.
<60%Low risk
60–80%Monitor closely — dew heater advised
>80%High fogging / dew risk
∆T Dew Point Margin
The gap between air temperature and dew point. When this closes to under 3°C, water condenses on any cool surface — your primary mirror or camera front element will fog. A dew heater is essential when ∆T is small.
>5°CSafe — no condensation risk
2–5°CDew heater strongly recommended
<2°CCondensation likely — optics at risk
👁 Visibility
Surface visibility reflects haze, smoke, dust, and aerosols in the lower atmosphere. Even without cloud, smoke or dust layers reduce contrast and limit how faint an object you can detect.
10+ kmExcellent transparency
5–10 kmSome haze — reduced contrast
<5 kmHeavy haze or fog — no-go
